About the people behind the tape

Who Made This?

Four people. A lot of feelings. One cassette.

Zoe
Side A — Curator
track 1–4

Zoe picked the songs that go first — the ones that ease you in before the gut-punch. She has this talent for sequencing that makes a playlist feel like a sentence with perfect grammar. Her rule: every tape starts with something that sounds like a window being opened.

I wanted the first track to feel like sunlight on your face before you've opened your eyes.
— Zoe, 2. 3. 2024

She wrote the label by hand, twice, because the first one smudged. The second one smudged a little too, but she decided that was better. More honest.

Dedicated to

"For anyone who needed a song at exactly the wrong moment — and found it."

Track 3 — her pick — is the one she said she almost didn't include. It stayed.
Marco
Side A — The wildcard
track 5–7

Nobody agreed with Marco's picks at first. By the third listen they were everyone's favourites. He has a nose for songs that feel wrong in the first ten seconds and inevitable by the chorus — the kind of track you can't explain to someone, only play at them.

A good song should make you feel slightly underdressed. Like you weren't ready for it.
— Marco, in a note on a napkin

He didn't write anything down for the label. He said the tape would speak for itself. He was right, but we're writing this down anyway because that's the kind of thing you want to have in a drawer somewhere twenty years from now.

Dedicated to

"To every song that got skipped on the first listen and loved on the third."

Rewind track 6 when you think it's over. There's a bit at the end he hid there.
Elena
Side B — The anchor
track 8–10

Elena took Side B. She said Side B is where tapes get serious — nobody flips a tape by accident. She picked songs that reward the effort of getting there. Her tracks are longer, slower, more likely to make you stare at the ceiling.

Side B is a commitment. I wanted to make it worth keeping.
— Elena, August

She's the one who found the instrumental for track 9 — a fifteen-second piano loop from a record nobody's heard of, from a town nobody's been to. It sounds like something you remember but can't place. She said that's the whole point.

Dedicated to

"For the version of you that's willing to sit with something for longer than it's comfortable."

Track 9 is best heard with headphones and eyes closed. She was very specific about this.
Riley
Side B — The closer
track 11–13

Riley ends the tape. They've always been good at endings — the kind that don't try to wrap everything up but still feel complete. Their last track fades out over thirty-two seconds, and nobody agreed on whether to cut it shorter. Nobody did.

An ending should leave room for what you're about to think. Not fill it.
— Riley, scribbled on the inside of the case

The handwriting on the back of the insert is Riley's. They were the only one whose writing was legible enough, and small enough to fit. They said they didn't want credit for it. It's credited to them anyway. Some things are too careful not to acknowledge.

Dedicated to

"To the drive home after. To the part of the night that's yours alone."

Turn up the volume before track 13. Trust the ending. It earns it.

This tape was made across four cities, a lot of voice notes, and one very long shared document none of us want to re-read. It took three months and one argument about track order that turned out to not matter at all.

We made it for you. Specifically you — even if we didn't know your name yet.
